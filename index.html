The One Shot That Changed Cinema

Visual innovations that permanently altered what filmmaking is capable of.

A handful of decisions by individual directors and cinematographers have fundamentally altered what cinema is capable of — moments when someone pointed a camera in a way no one had before, and something new came into the world.

Orson Welles's use of deep focus in Citizen Kane enabled compositions of extraordinary density — scenes where multiple planes of action unfolded simultaneously, the viewer's eye free to move within the frame rather than being directed. Jean-Luc Godard's jump cuts in Breathless broke the rule that explicitly forbade cutting between shots of the same subject from similar angles, creating a rhythm that felt simultaneously jagged and propulsive. What all these innovations share is a willingness to ask why: why does the camera have to be there? Why does the cut have to happen then? The history of cinema is largely the history of that question.

Breaking the Frame: When Rule-Breaking Creates Extraordinary Art

The difference between breaking rules with intention and breaking them by accident.

Every art form has its rules, and every set of rules exists for reasons — to solve problems of coherence, to create conditions for communication. The filmmaker who violates the 180-degree rule without knowing it produces confusion. The filmmaker who violates it knowing exactly what they are doing — and what the violation will communicate — produces something else: disorientation, alienation, the formal expression of a world gone wrong.

Alfonso Cuaron's Children of Men provides a masterclass. The film's long, uncut action sequences — some lasting ten minutes — violate every conventional wisdom about editing and tension. They should feel slow. Instead, they feel terrifyingly immediate. The absence of editing removes the safety net that cutting provides, trapping the viewer in continuous time alongside the characters. The director who understands their tools deeply enough to choose which rules to follow is working at the highest level of craft.

The Invisible Art of Film Editing

Why editing is the most decisive and least celebrated creative role in cinema.

Walter Murch, editor of Apocalypse Now, observed that a film is made three times: once when written, once when shot, and once when edited. The third making, he argues, is the most decisive — and the most invisible to audiences, which is either the measure of its success or the reason it is so chronically undervalued.

Editing is the art of choosing, from everything that was shot, what the film actually is. The language of editing is invisible when successful — we are not aware of the cut that takes us from an establishing shot to a close-up, from one time to another. Nothing in human perceptual experience actually works this way. Murch offers six criteria for a good cut, in order of priority: emotion, story, rhythm, eye trace, two-dimensional plane, three-dimensional space. Of these, emotion is by far the most important — a cut that serves the emotional truth of the scene can violate every other criterion and still feel right.
